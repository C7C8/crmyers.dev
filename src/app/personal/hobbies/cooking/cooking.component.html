"Smash burgers" are simple to make: take a griddle or a pan and heat it up as hot as you're comfortable going (I get mine to about 450 or so), lightly sprinkle a bit of vegetable oil on to get it started, then put a 1/4 lb ball of ground beef mix on and smash it down into the pan with a rigid spatula or a grill press. When making mix for the patties, I usually start with 1-2 lbs of ground beef (fattier is better here), a quarter cup or so of bread crumbs, some salt, pepper, Worcestershire sauce, and then I add any other mixins I feel like (e.g. smoked paprika, cheese, or onion powder.

Flip after about a minute or two of cooking, put cheese on the upturned side, cook for another minute, and you're done! The hotter your pan is the less you have to cook them to achieve a nice browned, nigh-crispy taste. If you want medium rare and moderately thin burgers, get your pan to scalding hot and cook fast! I highly recommend a good cast-iron grill press for the initial smash, but be wary of leaving it on while cooking or you run the risk of squishing the juice out of the burgers.

 Achieving a really good pan pizza isn't a specific recipe per se, but a series of tips of how to make that recipe you always go to really pop.

Dough

You can use most any dough recipe (I highly recommend the ones in the aforementioned Flour Water Salt Yeast), but I would err on the side of lower hydration and a longer bulk ferment time, to add to the taste of the dough. One essential deviation from norm, though: add a LOT of garlic powder to your dough! For a 1500g recipe I usually add a 1-2 tbsp, to put it in perspective. It adds that extra missing flavor you always taste at excellent pizza parlours. Don't be afraid to scatter a tiny bit on the crust of the pizza too, after you put the toppings on. Basil and rosemary are also excellent additions to a good dough if you want to make it more herbal.

Sauce

Don't buy your sauce pre-made, make it from scratch! It's much easier than you think. My simple starter for pizza sauce begins with olive oil on the bottom of my saucepan, which I sautee a few minced cloves of garlic, 1 tbsp of basil and 1 tbsp of oregano in, until it smells very fragrent. Then I add about 28oz of canned tomato sauce and simmer for an hour or two, with about 0.5 tbsp of salt and a few dashes of sugar (to neutralize acidity a bit).

Cheese

I used to avoid adding mozzarella to my pizza because I typically don't like the stuff, but I've learned its properties during baking are important. Typically I go for a blend of mozzarella, cheddar, and parmesan.

Baking

A well-seasoned cast iron pan should be fine with cornmeal instead of olive oil rubbed on the bottom, so I highly recommend that instead. Cornmeal is not only healthier (less oil) but gives the crust on the bottom a much better texture in my opinion, as it won't have a "fried"-esque effect to it.

Set your oven to the absolute hottest temperature it can go, and place your pizza around 6 inches beneath the top burner. Cook for about ten minutes, then put the broiler on HIGH and cook for another 2-3 minutes. The broiler adds a nice darkened crust on top and brings out a lot of extra flavor you didn't even know was there; it'll do marvellous things to pepperoni (see picture on left) if you let it!
